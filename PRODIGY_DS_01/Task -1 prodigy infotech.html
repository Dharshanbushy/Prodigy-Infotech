Visualizations for categorical variables¶

In [32]:
# Set the style for seaborn plots
sns.set(style="whitegrid")

# Education
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
ax = sns.countplot(data=data, x='Education', palette='Set2')
plt.title('Distribution of Education')
plt.xlabel('Education')
plt.ylabel('Count')
plt.xticks(rotation=45)

# Add annotations to each bar
for p in ax.patches:
    ax.annotate(format(p.get_height(), '.0f'), 
                (p.get_x() + p.get_width() / 2., p.get_height()), 
                ha = 'center', va = 'center', 
                xytext = (0, 10), 
                textcoords = 'offset points')

Based on the analysis of the categorical variables:¶

  1. Education Distribution:

    • The bar chart indicates that the most common educational attainment in the population is a Bachelor's degree, followed by Master's degrees and then PhDs.
    • The chart only provides counts, making it impossible to determine the percentage of the population with each level of education or the specific field of study.

  2. Gender Distribution:

    • The bar chart shows that there are more females than males in the represented population.
    • It's important to note that the exact count for each gender is not labeled on the y-axis.

  3. EverBenched Distribution:

    • The chart suggests that the percentage of individuals who have ever been benched is higher than those who have never been benched.
    • However, precise percentages cannot be determined from the graph alone.

In summary, while these visualizations provide insights into the distribution of categorical variables in the dataset, they have limitations. To gain a more comprehensive understanding, it would be beneficial to calculate percentages and possibly explore additional variables such as field of study or reasons for benching.
